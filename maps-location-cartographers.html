Maps, Location, and Cartographers

Maps of Arizona

1. Ancient Maps of the Southwest

Context: This map illustrates early European cartographic representations of the American Southwest, including what is now Arizona, from the 16th and 17th centuries. These maps were created by Spanish explorers and missionaries who documented their travels through the region. The cartography reflects limited knowledge of the interior geography, often showing the area as part of New Spain (Nueva España) with tentative river systems and mountain ranges. These early maps were crucial for Spanish colonial expansion and trade route establishment.

2. Trade Routes and Historical Interactions

Context: This map displays the complex network of trade routes that connected Arizona to neighboring regions throughout history. It shows ancient Native American trading paths used by the Ancestral Puebloans, Hohokam, and other cultures to exchange goods such as turquoise, pottery, and agricultural products. The map also illustrates Spanish mission trails, the Camino Real (Royal Road), and later routes used during the Mexican period and American territorial expansion. These routes facilitated cultural exchange and economic interactions between Arizona, Mexico, California, and other southwestern territories.

3. Continental Position and Latitudinal Range

Context: This map shows Arizona's geographical location within North America and its latitudinal extent. Arizona is situated in the southwestern United States, bordered by California and Nevada to the west, Utah to the north, New Mexico to the east, and Mexico to the south. The state spans approximately from 31°20'N (southern border with Mexico) to 37°N (northern border with Utah), placing it in the mid-latitude zone. This latitudinal position contributes to Arizona's diverse climate, ranging from desert conditions in the south to alpine environments in the northern highlands.

4. Modern Topographic Map (Albers Equal Area Conic Projection)

Context: This modern map of Arizona uses the Albers Equal Area Conic projection, which is well-suited for mapping the United States as it preserves area accuracy and minimizes distortion for mid-latitude regions. The map displays Arizona's diverse topography, including the Colorado Plateau in the north, the Basin and Range Province in the south, the Mogollon Rim, and major geographic features such as the Grand Canyon, Sonoran Desert, and San Francisco Peaks. This projection is commonly used by the U.S. Geological Survey for mapping the southwestern United States.

5. Boundary Changes Over Time

Context: This map illustrates the territorial evolution of Arizona from Spanish colonial times through statehood. It shows the shifting boundaries of New Spain, the Mexican territory of Alta California, the Gadsden Purchase of 1853-1854 (which added the southern portion of present-day Arizona), and the formation of the Arizona Territory in 1863. The map also depicts how Arizona's boundaries were adjusted during the territorial period before achieving statehood in 1912. These boundary changes reflect political negotiations, land purchases, and administrative decisions that shaped the modern state.

Location and Geographic Information

1. Continental Location and General Area

Arizona is located on the continent of North America, in the southwestern United States. The state is positioned in the interior of the North American continent, approximately 400-500 miles inland from the Pacific Coast. Arizona is part of the larger region known as the American Southwest, which includes parts of California, Nevada, Utah, Colorado, New Mexico, and Texas. The state occupies a strategic position between the Pacific Coast and the interior plains, making it an important corridor for transportation and migration throughout history.

2. Latitudinal Range

Arizona spans a latitudinal range from approximately 31°20'N to 37°N. The southernmost point of Arizona is located along its border with Mexico, near the town of Nogales, at approximately 31°20'N latitude. The northernmost point is at the state's border with Utah, near the town of Page, at approximately 37°N latitude. This span of roughly 5.67 degrees of latitude (approximately 390 miles) places Arizona entirely within the mid-latitudes of the Northern Hemisphere. This latitudinal range contributes to the state's diverse climates and ecosystems, from Sonoran Desert in the south to high-elevation forests and alpine environments in the north.

3. Time Periods of Existence

Arizona has existed as a distinct political entity in several forms throughout history:

  • Pre-Columbian Period (c. 12,000 BCE - 1540 CE): The region was inhabited by various Native American cultures, including the Ancestral Puebloans (Anasazi), Hohokam, Mogollon, and later the Apache, Navajo, Hopi, and Tohono O'odham nations.
  • Spanish Colonial Period (1540-1821): The area was part of New Spain and later the Spanish province of Sonora y Sinaloa.
  • Mexican Period (1821-1848): Following Mexican independence, the region was part of the Mexican states of Sonora and Alta California.
  • U.S. Territorial Period (1848-1912): After the Mexican-American War and the Gadsden Purchase (1853-1854), the area became part of the New Mexico Territory and then the separate Arizona Territory in 1863.
  • Statehood (1912-Present): Arizona achieved statehood on February 14, 1912, becoming the 48th state of the United States.

4. Famous Cartographers

Father Eusebio Francisco Kino (1645-1711)

Father Eusebio Francisco Kino was a Jesuit missionary, explorer, and cartographer who made significant contributions to the mapping of the American Southwest, including present-day Arizona. Born in Italy, Kino arrived in New Spain in 1681 and spent the remainder of his life exploring and mapping the region known as Pimería Alta (present-day southern Arizona and northern Sonora, Mexico).

Cartographic Accomplishments: Kino's most significant cartographic achievement was proving that Baja California was a peninsula, not an island as previously believed. He created detailed maps of the region, documenting the geography, river systems, and Native American settlements. His maps were among the first accurate European representations of the interior of the American Southwest. Kino's work included mapping the Gila and Colorado Rivers and establishing mission routes that became important transportation corridors. His maps influenced subsequent Spanish exploration and colonization of the region and provided valuable geographical knowledge that would be used by later cartographers and explorers.

John Wesley Powell (1834-1902)

John Wesley Powell was an American geologist, explorer, and cartographer who played a crucial role in mapping the American West, particularly the Colorado River and Grand Canyon regions of Arizona. After losing his right arm in the Civil War, Powell led expeditions that significantly advanced geographic knowledge of the Southwest.

Cartographic Accomplishments: Powell's most famous achievement was leading the first documented expedition down the Colorado River through the Grand Canyon in 1869. He produced detailed maps and surveys of the Colorado River basin, documenting the geography, geology, and topography of northern Arizona. As the second director of the U.S. Geological Survey (1881-1894), Powell oversaw systematic mapping of the western United States. He created the "Powell Geographic Expedition" maps that were instrumental in understanding the arid lands of the American West. His work influenced water policy and land management in Arizona and the broader region, and his maps provided the foundation for later topographic mapping efforts.

Clarence King (1842-1901)

Clarence King was an American geologist and the first director of the U.S. Geological Survey (1879-1881). Although not exclusively focused on Arizona, King's work significantly influenced cartographic and geological understanding of the American West, including Arizona.

Cartographic Accomplishments: King led the Geological Exploration of the Fortieth Parallel, which included mapping and surveying large portions of the western United States. His systematic approach to geological and topographical mapping set standards that would be used in mapping Arizona and other western states. King's work established the foundation for the detailed topographic maps that the U.S. Geological Survey would produce throughout the late 19th and early 20th centuries, including comprehensive mapping of Arizona's diverse terrain.
